\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Theoretical contribution:}
The cleanest empirical signal we measured---a $+0.16$ multiple-choice gap on tagged facts versus untagged---did not survive transformation into autoregressive generation. We interpret this as a structural property of attention-augmented language models: stored memory K/V provide attention-time logit perturbations that can shift discrete classification but cannot consistently steer token-by-token sampling against a strong pretrained prior. This recognition--recall dissociation is, to our knowledge, the first time the cognitive-psychology phenomenon has been localized mechanistically in a frozen transformer. Two further conceptual contributions follow: the multi-cycle evaluation shows that single-cycle benchmarks systematically misrank continual-learning architectures, and the internal-validation-versus-external-recall discrepancy ($37/52$ candidates passed surprise-reduction while DRA stayed at floor) demonstrates that proxy self-grading can certify learning that is not externally testable.

\paragraph{Practical implications:}
For practitioners, three operational lessons follow. First, direct-write KV memory is a usable mechanism for \emph{recognition}-style tasks (classification, multiple-choice, similarity judgment) on a frozen backbone, but not for free-form generation; closing that gap requires retrieval-aware fine-tuning in the manner of Wu et al. \citep{wu2022memorizing}. Second, biologically-inspired safety mechanisms (EWC, clipping, interleaving, rollback) impose a real recall cost on a single pass but pay for themselves across cycles---SLEEP preserved base capability roughly twice as well as naive LoRA at every cycle. Third, any continual-learning system should be reported on a multi-cycle protocol; a single-cycle per-cost comparison would have rejected the architecture that the multi-cycle data vindicates.

\paragraph{Challenges:}
Several scope constraints bound the strength of our claims:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.4em,itemsep=0.15em,topsep=0.2em]
    \item[(i)] We test a \emph{single} model scale (Qwen2.5-7B) and one dtype (bfloat16); the precision-floor finding for $\alpha_{\mathrm{slow}}$ is dtype-specific, and other findings may scale differently.
    \item[(ii)] We test \emph{synthetic} facts in 10 templates; natural document content may differ.
    \item[(iii)] We test a \emph{single} LoRA configuration (rank 16, V/O projections, top-third layers); other adapter geometries may behave differently.
    \item[(iv)] We force \emph{eager attention}; SDPA and FlashAttention paths handle 4D additive masks differently and we did not characterize them after the gating mechanism was finalized.
    \item[(v)] Our multi-cycle test runs \emph{three} cycles; longer continual-learning timescales may further differentiate the architectures.
    \item[(vi)] We use a \emph{single} random seed per condition; small effects carry run-to-run variance we did not characterize directly.
\end{itemize}

We can, however, bound the seed-variance question with a back-of-the-envelope power calculation. The headline recognition signal is a difference of two proportions: tagged-fact MC accuracy ($p_T = 0.28$, $n_T = 134$) versus untagged ($p_U = 0.12$, $n_U = 66$). Treating each fact as an independent Bernoulli trial, the standard error of the difference is $\sqrt{p_T(1-p_T)/n_T + p_U(1-p_U)/n_U} \approx \sqrt{0.0015 + 0.0016} \approx 0.056$. The observed effect of $+0.16$ is therefore approximately $2.9$ standard errors above zero (one-sided $z$-test, $p \approx 0.002$). A second seed could plausibly land $\pm 0.06$ from the observed mean, but the qualitative conclusion---a positive recognition signal of order $0.10$--$0.20$---should be robust. The Pareto curve and multi-cycle BCP results, where the absolute differences are larger ($\Delta\mathrm{BCP} > 1.0$ between SLEEP-A and naive LoRA at every cycle), have wider safety margins. We acknowledge that this is a back-of-the-envelope analysis, not multi-seed empirical confirmation, and treat it as a budget item for follow-up work. The strongest claim our evidence supports is: in this specific configuration, on this dataset, with these safety hyperparameters, we observe the patterns reported.

\paragraph{Ethical considerations:}
Continual-learning systems for language models, if they work, would reduce retraining costs substantially and enable models to learn from interaction. They would also raise privacy and safety concerns: persistent memory of user inputs, model memorization of sensitive information, and asymmetric power between systems that can update themselves and users who cannot. Our specific architecture stores user inputs (tagged spans) in the model's KV bank and consolidates a subset into permanent weights; deployed naively, this would amount to silent on-device data retention. We argue that any deployed continual-learning system must include explicit user controls over what is encoded, what is consolidated, and what is permanent, alongside mechanisms for selective forgetting on user request. These concerns are real but tractable; they do not, in our view, block the research direction.

\section{Conclusion and Future Scope}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We implemented and empirically evaluated a biologically-faithful memory consolidation architecture for transformer language models. Our experiments show that direct-write KV memory injection produces a measurable recognition signal in pretrained transformers but does not transfer to free-form recall without further mechanism; that the proposed consolidation step has no efficient single-cycle operating point yet does deliver multi-cycle preservation advantages over naive fine-tuning; and that five specific assumptions in the original formalization required empirical correction before the architecture would function at scale. The key open problem is consolidation: bridging from attention-based recognition into weights that support autoregressive generation, without sacrificing base-model preservation.

\paragraph{Future scope:}
Our results give follow-up work specific targets rather than vague directions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.4em,itemsep=0.15em,topsep=0.2em]
    \item[(i)] \emph{Retrieval-aware consolidation}: train the model to use stored memory during generation, in the manner of Memorizing Transformers, but as a phase within a multi-cycle consolidation pipeline rather than as a one-time fine-tune.
    \item[(ii)] \emph{External validation criteria}: move from surprise-reduction (a proxy of learning) to direct recall accuracy (the target) inside the consolidation loop.
    \item[(iii)] \emph{Adaptive safety mechanisms}: engage safety constraints only when degradation is detected, in the manner of trust-region methods, rather than applying them uniformly.
    \item[(iv)] \emph{Multi-cycle evaluation at longer timescales}: run well beyond three cycles to test whether SLEEP's BCP stabilizes or continues to compound.
\end{itemize}

We release the implementation, formalization, and full experimental logs to enable that work.
